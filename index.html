The thesis

The value is in the overlap.

Senior legal teams increasingly need one lawyer who can do several jobs at once: hold deep employment judgment, work hands-on with the AI systems the workforce now runs on, litigate when it matters, and still build the process that lets the business move. That overlap is where I do my best work.

Multi-jurisdictional reach

My employment practice has never been confined to one state. I have advised, litigated, and built compliant workforce systems across major jurisdictions including California, New York, Illinois, Michigan, Georgia, Texas, and other states — with current work supporting a national technology workforce and prior all-50-state client counseling. I am open to relocation or remote roles.

Employment and litigation depth

I have practiced labor and employment law across counseling, investigations, agency matters, litigation, arbitration, policy, training, and federal appellate defense, including a D.C. Circuit NLRB matter. The depth is tested across three operating modes: BigLaw advisor and litigator, in-house counsel, and founder.

Hands-on with the tools

I stay close to the tools themselves: frontier models, local LLMs, retrieval systems, and agentic workflows. That lets me see risks and adoption blockers many lawyers miss: how data enters, how outputs are used, how humans rely on the system, and where compliance exposure appears.

Scale and systems

At Walmart, I apply that employment experience and hands-on AI work to enterprise systems that, once deployed, can reach associates across the company. The work includes NIST-aligned questionnaires, compliance frameworks, model and product review, automated-decision-system analysis, privacy issue spotting, and employment AI compliance.

Executive translation

At Rocket, I co-led labor and employment through the company’s IPO, counseling leaders in real time and then building the policies, handbooks, agreements, and processes that helped a 30,000-person workforce run on the answer.
